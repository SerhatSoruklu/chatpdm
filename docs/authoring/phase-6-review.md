## Concept Outcomes

### `authority`

Final decision: `Pass`

Key weaknesses found and corrected:

- early wording risked collapsing authority into recognized power without making standing primary
- initial boundary language did not separate authority strongly enough from legitimacy

Corrections made:

- made recognized standing the center of the short definition
- made fullDefinition distinguish standing from validity and force

Remaining risk:

- moderate risk of confusion with legitimacy if future concepts weaken the standing versus validity distinction

### `power`

Final decision: `Pass`

Key weaknesses found and corrected:

- early wording risked making power sound too broad and unstructured
- initial relation language needed stronger contrast with authority and legitimacy

Corrections made:

- anchored power to effective capacity
- made explicit that power does not depend on acceptance, justification, or office

Remaining risk:

- low-to-moderate risk of over-expansion if future authoring treats all structural effect as power by default

### `legitimacy`

Final decision: `Pass`

Key weaknesses found and corrected:

- early phrasing risked treating legitimacy as authority with positive approval
- initial fullDefinition needed a sharper evaluative function

Corrections made:

- defined legitimacy as accepted validity rather than standing itself
- stated clearly that legitimacy evaluates standing rather than conferring capacity

Remaining risk:

- moderate risk of collapse into authority if later concepts blur recognized standing and valid standing

### `responsibility`

Final decision: `Pass`

Key weaknesses found and corrected:

- early phrasing risked making responsibility sound like owed conduct rather than answerability
- initial contexts were too close to accountability language

Corrections made:

- anchored responsibility to attribution plus answerability
- kept responsibility on the side of what can be called to account

Remaining risk:

- low risk if accountability is authored later without careful separation

### `duty`

Final decision: `Pass`

Key weaknesses found and corrected:

- early phrasing risked making duty sound like responsibility under another name
- initial structure needed a clearer before-action versus after-attribution split

Corrections made:

- anchored duty to required conduct owed under a binding source
- stated explicitly that broader responsibility is a further question

Remaining risk:

- low-to-moderate risk if future authoring turns duty into generic moral obligation without structural source

## Pairwise Review

### `authority` vs `power`

Why they do not collapse:

- `authority` is structured standing to direct
- `power` is effective capacity to produce effects

Separation mechanism:

- authority requires recognized standing within structure
- power does not require recognition, acceptance, or office

Remaining risk:

- moderate only if later authoring weakens the standing requirement in authority

### `authority` vs `legitimacy`

Why they do not collapse:

- `authority` is the standing or right itself
- `legitimacy` is the accepted validity of that standing, rule, or claim

Separation mechanism:

- authority asks who may direct
- legitimacy asks whether that standing counts as valid

Remaining risk:

- highest pairwise risk in the batch because both concepts involve recognized order and can drift together if not tightly maintained

### `responsibility` vs `duty`

Why they do not collapse:

- `responsibility` links actor to answerability
- `duty` links actor to required conduct

Separation mechanism:

- responsibility centers attribution and answerability
- duty centers owed conduct under rule, role, or obligation

Remaining risk:

- low if the answerability versus required-conduct split remains explicit

### `power` vs `legitimacy`

Why they do not collapse:

- `power` concerns operative effect
- `legitimacy` concerns accepted validity

Separation mechanism:

- power can operate without acceptance
- legitimacy concerns whether a standing, rule, or claim counts as valid

Remaining risk:

- low-to-moderate if later authoring assumes effective rule automatically carries legitimacy

## Batch Assessment

The five-concept batch is strong enough to proceed toward runtime later.

Reasons:

- the concepts are structurally comparable
- the critical pairs remain distinct under review
- the concept files use a controlled and consistent voice
- aliases remain minimal and do not attempt to rescue weak meaning

The main remaining caution is not within the current files themselves. It is future drift. The highest sensitivity remains the `authority` and `legitimacy` boundary.
